**[Slide 1 — Design is code (discernment required)]**

Good morning everyone. It's great to see so many of you here for the Figma Design Challenge. You've been spending the last few months doing something that, when I started my career, required an entire company to do badly. I'll explain what I mean.

**[Slide 2 — The conversation, → to reveal each bubble]**

When we started Tinkertanker, we were a dev shop. Clients came to us with ideas, and we wrote the code. Simple arrangement, you'd think. Here's how it actually went.

*(→)* A client would say, "we want a content management system."
*(→)* We'd nod, go away for [three months], and come back proud of ourselves: "here's the system we wrote for you."
*(→)* And the client would look at the screen, look at us, and say, "that's nothing like what we wanted. Redo it."
*(→)* Yeah. That was us.

**[Slide 3 — The wrong product]**

The problem wasn't the code. The code worked. The problem was that we were speaking two different languages. They said "system" and pictured one thing; we heard "system" and built another. Neither side had a way to point at something and say, this, exactly this. So we delivered, on time and on budget, exactly the wrong product.

The gap between what someone imagines and what actually gets built has been the central problem of software for decades. And for most of that time, the people with the ideas, the designers, the ones who could picture the right product, couldn't build it themselves. They had to describe it to people like me, and hope.

That's what something like Figma aims to address, and I hope you learned something from it.

**[Slide 4 — → "Average code works" / → "Average design doesn't"]**

At the same time, the tools have changed. AI can write code now.

*(→)* I want to say something that sounds a bit rude to my own profession: that's fine. AI generates average code, and average code works. If your loop is a bit inelegant, if your function is named something boring, nobody using your app will ever know. Average code compiles, runs, and does the job.

*(→)* Average design is a different story, because everyone sees it. Dylan Field, the CEO of Figma, puts it this way: the first thing AI gives you is generic by definition, because it's the average of everything it's seen. In code, average is a passing grade. In design, average is invisible at best and off-putting at worst.

**[Slide 5 — Spot the slop, → to reveal each callout]**

And you can see it everywhere now, because AI-generated design has tells. Have a look at this. Looks professional, right? Now look closer.

*(→ eyebrow)* That tiny label in capital letters above the heading? Designers call it eyebrow text, and AI puts one on everything.

*(→ serif title)* The dramatic serif headline, with one phrase in italics for extra drama, on a tech product that has no reason to be elegant.

*(→ mic drop)* And the writing! Every AI paragraph ends by dropping the mic. "This isn't just a to-do list. It's a revolution."

*(→ live indicator)* That little pulsing green dot that says LIVE, on a page where nothing is live.

*(→ left rails)* And coloured rails down the left of everything, whether it means anything or not.

None of these is wrong on its own. What's wrong is that they show up regardless of what's being designed. They're not choices. They're defaults. And design is the art of making choices. Hold onto that word — slop — because I want to show you its opposite.

**[Slide 6 — grug / Designers ship now]**

This is grug. Earlier this year it won an Apple Design Award, and it was built by two designers — Jip van der Velde and Michele Lings — who wrote the code themselves with an AI assistant. Look at those screens. No eyebrow text, no left rails, nothing generic about it. Every choice is theirs. That's what taste looks like when it ships.

And they're not the exception any more. Figma says 60% of designs on their platform are now made by people who aren't designers by job title. One tech CEO even said AI coding barely changed his engineering teams, but completely transformed his design teams, because suddenly they could ship.

After they won, one of them wrote a short thread about how they actually did it. I want to give you the last few minutes in their words, because they say it better than I could.

**[Slide 7 — grug, in their words: slop vs soul]**

Remember the tells from a moment ago — the eyebrow text, the rails, the mic-drop copy. Here's what grug's makers say about why grug is none of that.

*(read the quote)* They spent days and nights obsessing over every detail, every animation, every tiny bit of weirdness. And that, they say, is the difference between slop and something with a soul. The tells are what you get when nobody cared enough to make a choice. Soul is what you get when someone did.

**[Slide 8 — grug, in their words: taste]**

This next one is the heart of it, and it's basically the whole talk in three lines.

*(read the quote)* AI can write your code. It can make impossible things feel possible. But it cannot care — it can't make your app memorable, it can't give it a soul. You have to bring the taste. And notice the instruction hiding in there: throw away good-enough work to get to your best work. That's the muscle to train today — being willing to bin something that already runs, because you can tell it isn't yet right. That "isn't yet right" is your taste talking. Learn to hear it.

**[Slide 9 — grug, in their words: bolder / weirder]**

And here's what changes once the tools are this good.

*(read the quote)* You get bolder. You stop killing ideas just because they sound too hard to build. You get weirder, because trying things becomes cheap enough to be silly again. For fifteen years, their best ideas died as mockups — too weird, too small, too unlikely to survive a meeting. Not any more. So don't come into today with the sensible idea. Come in with the weird one, the small one, the one that would never survive a meeting.

**[Slide 10 — Now go make the weird thing / Thank you]**

So here's the ask. Use the tools for everything average — the boilerplate, the plumbing, the parts nobody will ever notice. Then spend what they hand back to you on the part that only you can do: care. Bring the taste. Throw away the good-enough. Make the weird thing — the right people will find it.

Have a great challenge. Thank you.
